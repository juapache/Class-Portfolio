# Lesson 1: Intro to Peace Innovation

**Duration:** 120 minutes (lecture + group discussion)  
**Level:** Undergraduate/Graduate  
**Language:** English (Spanish translation in progress)

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

- Define peace innovation and distinguish it from traditional peacebuilding and development
- Understand the unique challenges and opportunities of innovating in conflict contexts
- Apply design thinking and human-centered approaches to peace challenges
- Recognize emerging fields where innovation supports sustainable peacebuilding
- Develop an initial concept for a peace innovation project

---

## Section 1: What is Peace Innovation?

### Defining Peace Innovation

**Peace innovation** refers to new ideas, processes, products, or services designed to prevent conflict, reduce violence, and promote sustainable peace. It bridges multiple disciplines:

- **Peace Studies:** Understanding conflict dynamics, peacebuilding frameworks
- **Design & Technology:** User-centered design, digital solutions, systems thinking
- **Development:** Addressing root causes—poverty, inequality, lack of opportunity
- **Social Entrepreneurship:** Creating scalable, sustainable solutions
- **Community-Based Practice:** Local knowledge, participation, agency

Unlike commercial innovation (which aims for profit) or humanitarian aid (which responds to crises), peace innovation focuses on **transforming the conditions that enable conflict**.

### The Innovation Challenge in Peace Work

Peace innovation differs from other innovation contexts in critical ways:

| Dimension | Commercial Innovation | Peace Innovation |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------|
| **Stakeholders** | Investors, consumers | Affected communities, conflict parties, government |
| **Risk** | Market failure | Unintended escalation of conflict; harming vulnerable populations |
| **Timeline** | Quarterly/annual cycles | Years or decades |
| **Success Metrics** | Revenue, market share | Reduced violence, social cohesion, justice, sustainability |
| **Feedback Loops** | Immediate customer feedback | Delayed, indirect, context-dependent |

**Core Challenges:**

1. **Context Sensitivity:** Solutions must account for local histories, power dynamics, cultural factors, and political economy
2. **Risk of Harm:** Well-intentioned interventions can inadvertently escalate tensions, benefit elites while marginalizing others, or undermine local agency
3. **Complexity:** Peace challenges involve multiple stakeholders with competing interests, asymmetric power, and historical trauma
4. **Long-term Commitment:** Peace innovation requires patience and sustained engagement beyond typical project cycles

### Design Thinking as a Foundation

**Design thinking** is a human-centered problem-solving approach that emphasizes:

1. **Empathy:** Deep understanding of user needs, constraints, aspirations, and contexts
2. **Problem Definition:** Clarifying the real problem (not the assumed solution)
3. **Ideation:** Generating many ideas without judgment
4. **Prototyping:** Building rough versions quickly to test assumptions
5. **Iteration:** Learning from feedback and refining solutions

For peace innovation, design thinking offers:

- **Centering affected communities:** Rather than experts designing "for" people, communities become co-designers
- **Rapid learning:** Testing ideas in small scale before full implementation
- **Flexibility:** Adapting to changing contexts and new information
- **Integration:** Combining technological, social, institutional, and cultural dimensions

## Section 2: Conflict-Sensitive Innovation

### Understanding Conflict Sensitivity

Any innovation in conflict contexts must be **conflict-sensitive**—meaning it:

1. **Does No Harm:** Actively avoids reinforcing conflict dynamics or marginalizing vulnerable groups
2. **Understands Context:** Analyzes root causes, parties, historical grievances, power dynamics, and resource competition
3. **Respects Local Agency:** Acknowledges that affected communities are experts in their own contexts and must lead solutions
4. **Strengthens Cohesion:** Builds relationships, trust, and interdependence across divides rather than deepening divisions

### The "Do No Harm" Framework

Based on humanitarian and development ethics, **Do No Harm** asks innovators to:

**Questions to ask:**
- Could this innovation inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities or stereotypes?
- Might it benefit some groups while marginalizing others?
- Could conflict actors manipulate or weaponize it?
- Does it respect local values, decision-making, and priorities?
- Could it create dependency on external actors?

**Example:** A digital early warning system for conflict

*Potential harms:*
- Centralizes information with government, which might use it for surveillance or targeting minorities
- Excludes communities lacking internet access, creating information asymmetry
- Positions international "experts" as authorities, undermining local knowledge

*Conflict-sensitive redesign:*
- Communities co-design what counts as "warning signs" (not external definition)
- Multiple actors (civil society, local government, informal leaders) have access
- System strengthens existing community networks rather than replacing them

### Stakeholder Mapping and Engagement

Effective peace innovation requires understanding:

**Primary stakeholders (directly affected by conflict):**
- Communities experiencing violence (including marginalized subgroups: women, youth, disabled persons)
- Conflict parties and their constituencies
- Local civil society organizations

**Secondary stakeholders (influential but not directly affected):**
- Regional and national government
- International organizations
- Business and economic actors
- Religious and cultural leaders

**Key questions:**
- Who must be at the table for the solution to be legitimate and sustainable?
- Who has been historically excluded from peacebuilding decisions?
- What power dynamics exist, and how might they shape who benefits?
- How can decision-making be structured to ensure genuine participation, not tokenism?

## Section 3: Frameworks and Tools for Peace Innovation

### The Design Thinking Cycle (Detailed)

**1. Empathize (Research & Understanding)**
- Interviews, focus groups, observation in conflict communities
- Document needs, constraints, aspirations, informal institutions
- Map power dynamics, resource flows, existing solutions (formal and informal)

**2. Define (Problem Framing)**
- Synthesize research into a clear problem statement
- Avoid solutions that are already implied in the problem definition
- Example: Don't say "We need a mobile app to prevent conflict"; say "Community leaders lack a trusted channel to alert authorities to tensions before escalation"

**3. Ideate (Solution Generation)**
- Brainstorm without judgment
- Encourage "wild ideas" that might inspire feasible options
- Example ideas: Create a hotline, establish community liaison, train youth mediators, establish regular dialogue forums

**4. Prototype (Low-Fidelity Testing)**
- Build rough versions quickly (paper mockups, role-play, low-tech pilots)
- Test with small groups from the community
- Learn what works, what doesn't, why
- Cost: minimal; learning: maximum

**5. Test & Iterate**
- Gather feedback systematically
- Refine based on learning, not just user preferences
- Return to earlier stages if needed (you might redefine the problem after testing)

### The Peace Innovation Canvas

A structured tool similar to the Business Model Canvas that guides strategic thinking:

| Element | Questions |
|---------|-----------|
| **Problem & Context** | What is the peace challenge? What are root causes? Who experiences it? |
| **Stakeholders** | Who are primary stakeholders? Who are gatekeepers? What are their interests? |
| **Theory of Change** | How will this innovation reduce violence or build peace? What assumptions underlie it? |
| **Innovation Approach** | Technology? Social/institutional? Cultural/educational? Community-based? |
| **Resources & Constraints** | What do we have? What are barriers (financial, political, social, technical)? |
| **Partnerships & Scale** | Who must partner? How does it grow from pilot to sustainable scale? |
| **Measurement** | How will we know it's working? What are leading and lagging indicators? |

### Systems Thinking for Peace Innovation

Peace systems are complex, with multiple actors, feedback loops, and unintended consequences. Systems thinking helps innovators:

**Identify Relationships:**
- How do conflict parties interact? Are there opportunities for cooperation?
- Who benefits from current arrangements, and who loses?
- Where are alliances and divisions?

**Recognize Feedback Loops:**
- Reinforcing loops (escalation): An attack triggers retaliation, triggering more attacks
- Balancing loops (stabilization): International pressure reduces violence; reduced pressure allows escalation to resume

**Find Leverage Points:**
- Small changes in high-leverage areas can shift entire systems
- Example: Building trust between youth leaders across divides can shift community attitudes, reducing recruitment to violence

**Anticipate Unintended Consequences:**
- Innovation that reduces one form of violence might increase another
- Example: A job training program that includes only one ethnic group might increase grievances among the excluded

## Section 4: Emerging Fields and Applications

### Technology and Peace Innovation

**Early Warning Systems**
- Use conflict data, media monitoring, social media analysis to anticipate violence risks
- Examples: ACLED (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data), PREDICT (machine learning for conflict forecasting)
- Opportunities: Enable proactive prevention, early response
- Risks: Data reflects patterns of violence reporting (not actual violence); can legitimize heavy-handed government response

**Digital Dialogue Platforms**
- Online spaces for communication across conflict divides
- Examples: Coexist (platform for dialogue in Middle East), Divided We Fall (cross-partisan dialogue)
- Opportunities: Safe anonymity, reach beyond geographic limitations
- Risks: Echo chambers; platforms can be weaponized for misinformation; excludes those without digital access

**Monitoring and Mapping**
- Document violence, displacement, resource access
- Examples: Satellite imagery for early warning; participatory mapping of violence and services
- Opportunities: Evidence-based advocacy; hold perpetrators accountable
- Risks: Privacy concerns; can endanger sources if compromised

**Counter-Misinformation**
- Fact-checking, prebunking, narrative alternatives
- Examples: Initiatives in Myanmar, Sri Lanka, Kenya tracking COVID-misinformation that fuels ethnic tensions
- Opportunities: Address conflict narratives at scale
- Risks: Who defines "misinformation"? Can marginalize legitimate grievances

### Social Innovation and Community-Based Solutions

**Reconciliation and Accountability Programs**
- Truth commissions, restorative justice, trauma healing
- Examples: South African TRC, Rwanda's gacaca courts, Colombia's transitional justice mechanisms
- Innovation: Combining individual healing with social transformation

**Economic Initiatives**
- Creating livelihoods and interdependence across divides
- Examples: Cooperative enterprises, joint business ventures, value chains that require cross-community cooperation
- Innovation: Economic incentives for peace as complement to political negotiations

**Cultural and Creative Expression**
- Using arts, music, theater, storytelling for peace
- Examples: Concerts uniting musicians from conflict sides; theater addressing historical trauma; poetry as reconciliation
- Innovation: Reaching emotions and imagination beyond rational argumentation

**Educational Innovation**
- Teaching peace skills (communication, critical thinking, perspective-taking)
- Examples: Integrated schools in divided societies; curriculum reforming historical narratives; peace education in conflict regions
- Innovation: Building peace culture in the next generation

### Organizational and Institutional Innovation

**Inclusive Governance**
- Decision-making structures that include marginalized voices
- Examples: Quota systems for women and minorities; participatory budgeting; traditional and modern governance hybrids
- Innovation: Balancing representation with efficiency and legitimacy

**Dispute Resolution Mechanisms**
- Formal and informal systems for justice and conflict resolution
- Examples: Community courts, mediation services, hybrid courts combining traditional and international law
- Innovation: Accessibility, legitimacy, sustainability

**Public-Private Partnerships**
- Mobilizing business resources and innovation for peace
- Examples: Private sector job creation in post-conflict regions; corporate funding of peacebuilding
- Risks: Business interests may not align with peace; market solutions may deepen inequality

**Regional and International Frameworks**
- New agreements and institutions supporting peace
- Examples: Regional human rights courts; conflict early warning systems; transnational cooperation on shared challenges
- Innovation: Creating accountability and cooperation at scale beyond traditional state systems

## Section 5: From Learning to Action

### Case Study: Rwandan Genocide Survivors Fund

**Problem:** Rwanda's post-genocide economy marginalized genocide survivors and vulnerable youth, limiting pathways to livelihoods and dignity. High unemployment fueled tensions and criminality.

**Innovation Approach:** A community-based social enterprise model that:
- Supported survivors in starting small businesses (e.g., crafts, agriculture)
- Created employment for youth through training and job placement
- Built cooperation across survivor and perpetrator communities through joint ventures
- Generated income that funded reconciliation activities

**How it addressed peace challenges:**
- **Economic:** Created livelihoods, reducing recruitment to violence
- **Social:** Built trust through economic interdependence
- **Psychological:** Provided dignity and agency to survivors
- **Institutional:** Strengthened local economic governance

**Lessons:**
- Innovation required long-term support, not one-time grants
- Success depended on addressing power imbalances (survivors needed preferential access to resources)
- Sustainability required integrating with government and market systems, not operating in parallel

## Section 6: Designing Your Peace Innovation Project

To develop your own peace innovation concept:

### 1. Identify a Peace Challenge

Think of a conflict context you care about (local, regional, or global). What is a specific peace challenge?

Examples:
- Youth recruitment to extremism
- Communal land disputes turning violent
- Lack of accountability for sexual violence
- Broken relations preventing trade across divides

### 2. Understand Stakeholders

Who are the primary stakeholders? What are their perspectives, needs, and constraints?

**Exercise:** Draw a stakeholder map. Use different colors or symbols for different groups. Draw lines showing relationships (alliance, competition, subordination, connection).

### 3. Analyze Root Causes

Using systems thinking, what are the underlying structural, cultural, and behavioral causes? 

**Use Galtung's ABC Triangle:**
- **C (Contradiction):** What are the core incompatibilities? (territory, identity, resources, power, values)
- **B (Behavior):** What hostile or violent actions result?
- **A (Attitude):** What narratives, stereotypes, or grievances perpetuate the conflict?

### 4. Brainstorm Solutions Across Domains

Generate ideas in multiple domains:
- **Technological:** Digital tools, mobile apps, platforms
- **Organizational:** New institutions, decision-making structures, services
- **Educational:** Teaching peace skills, reforming narratives
- **Economic:** Creating interdependence, livelihoods, cooperation
- **Cultural/Artistic:** Using creative expression, ritual, storytelling

### 5. Prototype Your Best Idea

What is the smallest viable test of your innovation?

- Who is your first user group?
- What is the simplest version you could test?
- What do you most need to learn?
- What resources do you need?

### 6. Plan for Conflict Sensitivity

Apply Do No Harm:
- Who might be harmed by this innovation?
- Who might be excluded?
- What could conflict actors do with it?
- How will you ensure community leadership?

## Activities & Assignments

### Activity 1: Stakeholder Mapping (In-Class, 25 minutes)

**Instructions:**
1. Work in small groups of 3-4
2. Choose a conflict or peace challenge
3. Create a visual map of stakeholders:
   - Primary stakeholders (experienced violence directly)
   - Secondary stakeholders (influential actors)
   - Use arrows to show relationships and power dynamics
4. Label each actor's core interests in the conflict or peace process

**Deliverable:** Visual map on poster paper or digital format

### Activity 2: Design Thinking Rapid Prototype (Homework + In-Class, 60 minutes across two sessions)

**Homework (Before next class):**
1. Choose a peace challenge
2. Interview 2-3 people with lived experience of the challenge (can be informal conversations)
3. Synthesize: What did you learn about the problem? What surprised you?

**In-Class (Next session):**
1. Form teams around similar challenges
2. Develop a problem statement (10 minutes)
3. Brainstorm solutions (15 minutes)
4. Build a low-fidelity prototype using available materials: paper, string, cardboard, role-play (15 minutes)
5. Test with other teams (give feedback, not validation) (10 minutes)
6. Reflect: What did you learn? What would you change? (10 minutes)

**Deliverable:** Photo/video of prototype + 1-page reflection

## Instructor Notes

- This lesson introduces peace innovation as a distinct field combining peace studies, design, technology, and community practice
- Emphasize the "why" (challenges are complex and interdisciplinary) before the "how" (frameworks and tools)
- Use local case studies or invite local peacebuilders as guest speakers
- Encourage students to connect concepts to their own contexts and identities
- Frame peace innovation as both academic (scholarship) and practical (action)
- Prepare students for deep disagreement: There is no consensus on "best" approaches to peace, and context matters enormously
